NEW ZEALAND

Biculturalism refers to the presence of two different cultures in the same country or region. New Zealand is a country with two peoples: the existing Maori and the non-Maori who migrated. As a result, Maori culture and non-Maori culture existed together and in the early days of the migration of the non-Maori people, each culture was balanced within each culture . In 1840, New Zealand had only about 2,000 non-Maori people, while the Maori people were between 70,000 and 90,000, which was quite large. For this reason, the culture of the non-Maori people who migrated could not invade the existing Maori people's culture. For a long time since then, the Maori and non-Maori have respected each other's culture and even learned each other's culture. At this time, the non-Maori people who lived with the Maori and served as intermediaries between the two cultures were called Pākehā-Maori, and they became the first bicultural people. Some Maori chiefs even encouraged them to marry the Maori. However, this balance did not last long. The population of the non-Maori continued to increase and eventually became larger than the Maori. Treaty of Waitangi, which promises to protect the Maori and their culture, is a treaty signed by the native Maori and the King of England on the northern island of Waitangi. However, Britain later established a government in New Zealand and did not protect the Maori culture promised by the treaty of Waitangi They hoped the Maori would assimilate into the non-Maori culture, and they ignored the Maori culture in New Zealand for a long time and did not protect it at all. For example, they tried to relocate the Maori to a city where the culture of the non-Maori was dominant and there was no regulation to protect the Maori culture. So New Zealand has long been an informal monoculture until the Maori Renaissance in 1970. The Maori Renaissance is a social change made by Maori people and their supporters through protests that non-Maori violated the treaty of Waitangi. Representatively, in 1975, Dame Whinai Cooper marched with 5,000 people from Northland, traveled to the North Island, and arrived at Wellington to protest the loss of their land. Eventually, the British government accepted their protest and investigated whether Waitangi violated the treaty. Through this Maori Renaissance, society has changed. The government began to talk about New Zealand, where biculturalism would be balanced, They explained the treaty of Waitangi in the founding documents of the country. The non-Maori people protected the Maori culture again, and New Zealand changed and emphasized Biculturalism again New Zealand has changed again to balance the two cultures.However, the non-Maori and Maori continue to discuss and confront. Some Maori people argue that it's not enough biculturalism yet, They argue that efforts to protect Maori culture are insufficient and that non-Maori people need to make efforts to promote Maori culture and rights However, the non-Maori disagree with this argument, they said There are various cultures in New Zealand, and it is argued that it is too much to emphasize only Maori culture over other cultures.

The Treaty of Waitangi

The Treaty of Waitangi was signed on February 6, 1840, by England and native Maori. It is the most important document in New Zealand's history.  First of all, back in the late 1830s to look into the historical background of the Treaty. The number of Europeans moving to New Zealand has increased rapidly at that time. Around 1840, a Large group of Europeans more than 2000 settled in New Zealand, the newcomers between the Maori dispute went on without end. Accordingly, instead of mobilizing military power, the British government promoted the colonization of New Zealand through the signing of a treaty.  The British government appointed William Hobson as the Governor of New Zealand. And instructed him to transfer the rule of the Maori tribe leader to Queen Victoria and make to give the queen whole rights of the deal instead of guaranteeing the Maori land and resources.  46 of the tribal heads of the New Zealand Tribe Association signed the treaty of Waitangi on February 6, 1840. Since then, William Hobson has toured all over New Zealand and obtained signatures. As a result, more than 500 chiefs across the country signed the treaty of Waitangi excluding 39 chiefs of Waikato.  The Treaty of Waitangi is as follows.  The Treaty has three articles. In the English version,   Maori cede the sovereignty of New Zealand to Britain   Maori give the Crown an exclusive right to buy lands they wish to sell and, in return, are guaranteed full rights of ownership of their lands, forests, fisheries, and other possessions; and   Maori are given the rights and privileges of British subjects.  In general, the unilateral invasion and slaughter of ethnic groups with stronger military power have been repeated countless times in the history of colonization. The Treaty of Waitangi has the historic significance of the whole world because the two ethnic groups were united according to the document instead of the big stick.   On the other hand, The Treaty of Waitangi was written in the Maori version and the English version, and there was a difference in interpretation, which caused conflict.  Differences in interpretation are causing countless disputes over how to divide the power of Maori and white people and how far Maori ownership of land and resources will be recognised. For this reason, the Maori protested against Britain (Maori Renaissance), which led to the establishment of the "Waitangi Court" in 1975, and all disputes arising from differences in interpretation of the treaty were decided to be mediated through this court. Every February 6, 1974, it was designated as a national holiday called Waitangi Day. Through a peaceful colonial treaty that did not mobilize force, Maori culture and British culture in New Zealand now exist together. Therefore, when discussing the founding history of New Zealand, the treaty of Waitangi has an important significance that cannot be left out.
